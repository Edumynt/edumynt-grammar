## Introduction: Why "Ram as Well as Shyam Are" Is Usually Wrong

The phrase **as well as** looks simple, but it creates a serious subject-verb agreement trap.

> Incorrect: Ram as well as Shyam **are** coming.  
> Correct: Ram as well as Shyam **is** coming.

Many learners treat **as well as** like **and**. That is the mistake. **And** usually joins two subjects and makes the subject plural:

> Ram and Shyam **are** coming.

But **as well as** does not work exactly like **and**. It adds extra information. The verb normally agrees with the first, main subject.

> Ram, as well as Shyam, **is** coming.

This rule matters in school grammar, competitive exams, formal writing, and editing because the sentence often contains one singular noun and one plural noun. The nearby noun can distract you, but the verb should follow the real subject.

For related agreement traps, see [Subject-Verb Agreement with "One of"](/en/posts/subject-verb-agreement-one-of/) and [Collective Nouns: When "Team Is" and "Team Are" Both Work](/en/posts/collective-nouns-agreement/).

## Core Meaning and Rule

**As well as** means something close to **in addition to** or **along with**. It adds another person or thing, but it does not normally create a compound plural subject.

> The principal as well as the teachers **is** responsible.  
> The teachers as well as the principal **are** responsible.

The added phrase changes information, not the grammatical number of the main subject.

> **Rule box:** With **as well as**, the verb agrees with the first/main subject, not with the noun after **as well as**.

So the pattern is:

> singular subject + as well as + another noun + singular verb  
> plural subject + as well as + another noun + plural verb

Examples:

> **My brother**, as well as my parents, **is** invited.  
> **My parents**, as well as my brother, **are** invited.

The meaning is similar, but the grammar changes because the first subject changes.

## Forms, Patterns, and Sentence Position

### Main Patterns

| First/Main Subject | Added Phrase | Correct Verb | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| singular | as well as + singular noun | singular | **Ram as well as Shyam is** coming. |
| singular | as well as + plural noun | singular | **The box as well as the books was** lost. |
| plural | as well as + singular noun | plural | **The teachers as well as the principal are** present. |
| plural | as well as + plural noun | plural | **The boys as well as the girls are** ready. |

The first subject controls the verb.

### With Commas

In careful writing, the **as well as** phrase is often separated by commas, especially when it is extra information:

> My brother, **as well as my parents**, **is** invited.  
> The manager, **as well as the assistants**, **has** arrived.

The commas make the structure clearer: the middle phrase is additional, not the main subject.

Without commas, the rule is still usually the same:

> The manager as well as the assistants **has** arrived.

However, many modern writers prefer commas or a rewrite because the sentence can feel heavy.

### After a Plural First Subject

If the first subject is plural, use a plural verb:

> The teachers as well as the principal **are** present.  
> The files as well as the folder **were** deleted.  
> My friends as well as my cousin **have** registered.

Do not let the singular noun after **as well as** pull the verb into singular form.

### When You Really Mean "And"

If both people or things are equally part of the subject, **and** may be clearer:

> Ram and Shyam **are** coming.

If the second person is added as extra information, **as well as** is fine:

> Ram, as well as Shyam, **is** coming.

The choice is not only grammar; it affects emphasis.

## Decision Checklist

Use this method in error spotting and fill-in-the-blanks.

1. Find **as well as** in the sentence.
2. Identify the first/main subject before **as well as**.
3. Ignore the added phrase for verb agreement.
4. Choose the verb that agrees with the first subject.
5. Read the sentence again to check whether **and** would be more natural.

### Checklist Example 1

> Ram as well as Shyam is/are coming.

The first subject is **Ram**, singular. Correct:

> Ram as well as Shyam **is** coming.

### Checklist Example 2

> The teachers as well as the principal is/are present.

The first subject is **teachers**, plural. Correct:

> The teachers as well as the principal **are** present.

### Checklist Example 3

> The box as well as the books was/were lost.

The first subject is **box**, singular. Correct:

> The box as well as the books **was** lost.

## Examples That Show the Difference

1. **Ram as well as Shyam is** coming.  
   **Ram** is the first subject, so the verb is singular.

2. **Ram and Shyam are** coming.  
   **And** joins two subjects equally, so the verb is plural.

3. **The teachers as well as the principal are** present.  
   The first subject **teachers** is plural.

4. **The principal as well as the teachers is** present.  
   The first subject **principal** is singular.

5. **The box as well as the books was** lost.  
   The first subject **box** is singular.

6. **The books as well as the box were** lost.  
   The first subject **books** is plural.

7. **My brother, as well as my parents, is** invited.  
   The added phrase does not make the verb plural.

8. **My parents, as well as my brother, are** invited.  
   Now the first subject is plural.

9. **The captain as well as the players has** accepted the decision.  
   The main subject is **captain**.

10. **The players as well as the captain have** accepted the decision.  
   The main subject is **players**.

## Exam Traps and Usage Notes

### Trap 1: Treating "As Well As" Like "And"

> Incorrect: Ram as well as Shyam **are** coming.  
> Correct: Ram as well as Shyam **is** coming.

**And** creates a compound subject. **As well as** usually adds a secondary element. That is why the verb follows the first subject.

### Trap 2: The Nearest Noun Trap

> Incorrect: The box as well as the books **were** lost.  
> Correct: The box as well as the books **was** lost.

The nearest noun is **books**, but the main subject is **box**. Agreement follows the main subject, not the nearest noun.

This is the same broad skill used in many agreement questions: find the real subject before choosing the verb.

### Trap 3: Reversing the Subject Changes the Verb

> The principal as well as the teachers **is** present.  
> The teachers as well as the principal **are** present.

Both sentences include the same people, but the grammar is different because the first subject is different.

### Trap 4: Commas Do Not Change the Agreement

> Correct: My brother, as well as my parents, **is** invited.  
> Correct: My brother as well as my parents **is** invited.

Commas make the added phrase clearer, but the agreement logic is the same. In formal writing, commas often improve readability.

### Trap 5: Awkward but Grammatically Possible Sentences

Some sentences are technically correct but sound unnatural:

> The principal as well as all the teachers **is** coming.

The agreement is with **principal**, so **is** is correct. But if the writer wants to emphasize the whole group equally, this is cleaner:

> The principal and all the teachers **are** coming.

Grammar is not only about passing tests. Good writing should also sound natural and communicate emphasis clearly.

### BrE and AmE Note

The basic rule is the same in standard British and American English: **as well as** does not normally make the subject plural. In both varieties, formal writing prefers agreement with the first/main subject.

## Common Mistakes

|---|---|---|
| Ram as well as Shyam **are** coming. | Ram as well as Shyam **is** coming. | **Ram** is singular. |
| The teachers as well as the principal **is** present. | The teachers as well as the principal **are** present. | **Teachers** is plural. |
| The box as well as the books **were** lost. | The box as well as the books **was** lost. | **Box** is singular. |
| My brother, as well as my parents, **are** invited. | My brother, as well as my parents, **is** invited. | **Brother** is singular. |
| The files as well as the folder **was** missing. | The files as well as the folder **were** missing. | **Files** is plural. |

## Summary

**As well as** adds information, but it does not usually make the subject plural.

> **Rule box:** With **as well as**, the verb agrees with the first/main subject.

Memory trick:

> Cover the **as well as** phrase with your hand. The remaining subject tells you the verb.
