English grammar becomes easier when one confusing rule is separated from the noise. This lesson explains **Subject-Verb Agreement with Either Or and Neither Nor** in a practical exam-friendly way, with examples you can actually use in error spotting, fill in the blanks, and MCQs.

## Quick rule

With either...or and neither...nor, the verb often agrees with the subject nearer to it.

Keep this rule as the first filter. In most school and competitive exam questions, the wrong option breaks this basic idea before it breaks anything advanced.

## Why students get confused

Students usually get this wrong for three reasons:

1. They look at the nearest word instead of the word that controls the grammar.
2. They translate directly from Hindi or everyday speech.
3. They remember a shortcut but forget the exception or sound-based detail.

A useful habit is to slow down and ask: **What is the function of this word in the sentence?** Is it a subject, object, noun, verb, adjective, adverb, time marker, or fixed phrase?

## Correct usage

Use the rule in context:

- **Neither the teacher nor the students are late.**
- **Neither the students nor the teacher is late.**

If both examples look similar at first, focus on the grammar role. The correct answer is not always the option that sounds common in conversation. Exam English usually rewards standard usage.

## Wrong vs right examples

| Wrong | Right | Why |
|---|---|---|
| This sentence ignores the rule: *Neither the students nor the teacher is late.* used in the wrong place. | Neither the students nor the teacher is late. | The form matches the grammar role. |
| A common shortcut gives the wrong choice. | Neither the teacher nor the students are late. | The sentence follows the actual rule, not a half-remembered shortcut. |
| The nearby word tricks you. | Check the main subject or target noun first. | Agreement and reference depend on the controlling word. |

## Exam angle

In error spotting, this topic usually appears as a small change in one part of a sentence. Do not read only the underlined part. Read the complete sentence and check these points:

- What word is being described or controlled?
- Is the noun countable or uncountable, singular or plural, specific or general?
- Is the sentence formal exam English or casual speech?
- Does a time marker, article, preposition, or pronoun clue decide the answer?

**Common trap:** Look at the noun closest to the verb.

## Memory trick

Turn the rule into a two-second test: **identify the grammar role first, then choose the word.** If you choose by sound alone, you may pass easy questions but fail the tricky ones.

## Practice mini-quiz

Choose the correct option.

1. Which sentence follows the rule best?
   A. The sentence that ignores the controlling word.
   B. Neither the teacher nor the students are late.
   C. The sentence with a random matching word.
   **Answer: B**

2. What should you check first in this topic?
   A. The grammar role of the word
   B. The longest option
   C. The most familiar sound
   **Answer: A**

3. Which method is safest in exams?
   A. Translate word by word from Hindi
   B. Guess by speaking speed
   C. Apply the rule and then check meaning
   **Answer: C**

## Summary

With either...or and neither...nor, the verb often agrees with the subject nearer to it. Learn the rule, test it with examples, and then look for the exam trap. That is enough to solve most questions from this topic confidently.

## FAQs

### Is this rule always tested directly?

Not always. Many questions hide it inside a longer sentence, so you must identify the grammar role before choosing the option.

### Should I trust spoken English for this topic?

Use spoken English as a clue, not as final proof. Competitive exams usually expect standard written English.
