## Research / Grounding Notes

- **Core concept:** Both "since" and "for" express duration in perfect tenses, but "since" marks a **starting point** (a specific moment), while "for" marks a **duration** (a length of time).
- **Key rule:** since + point in time (since 2010, since Monday, since I was a child); for + period (for three years, for a long time, for ages).
- **References:** Quirk et al. §4.19, §8.57; Swan *Practical English Usage* (entries on since/for); Murphy *English Grammar in Use* (Units 7–10).
- **Common errors:** "since three years" → "for three years"; "for Monday" → "since Monday."
- **Exam relevance:** Very high — one of the most tested preposition pairs.
- **Overlap warning:** Related to #051 (present perfect vs past simple) — keep focused on since/for.

## Section Structure

### 1. Introduction: Two Words, Two Kinds of Time
- "I have lived here since 2015" vs "I have lived here for 8 years."
- Same tense, different time logic.

### 2. "Since" — The Starting Point
- "Since" answers: "From when?"
- Since + specific point: since 2020, since January, since last week, since I arrived, since childhood.
- Always used with a perfect tense (present perfect, past perfect, etc.).

### 3. "For" — The Duration
- "For" answers: "How long?"
- For + period: for two hours, for many years, for a while, for ages.
- Can be used with any tense: "I stayed for two hours" (past simple), "I have waited for days" (present perfect).

### 4. The Key Difference in One Sentence
- "She has worked here **since 2018**." (starting point — from 2018 until now)
- "She has worked here **for six years**." (duration — six years total)
- Both can describe the same situation — the perspective is different.

### 5. Common Mistakes
- "since three years" → "for three years"
- "for 2015" → "since 2015"
- "since a long time" → "for a long time"
- "I am living here since 2020" → "I have been living here since 2020" (needs perfect tense with "since")

### 6. "Since" as a Conjunction (Bonus)
- "Since" can also mean "because": "Since you're here, let's begin."
- Brief mention — don't confuse with the preposition use.

### 8. Summary
- Since = starting point (a moment). For = duration (a length). Both often go with present perfect.
