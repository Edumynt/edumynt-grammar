## Research / Grounding Notes

- **Core concept:** "Good" is an adjective (modifies nouns); "well" is an adverb (modifies verbs). Exception: "well" = "in good health" (adjective).
- **Key rule:** She is good (adjective, describes "she"). She sings well (adverb, describes "sings").
- **References:** Quirk et al. §7.7–7.9; Swan *Practical English Usage* (entry on good/well); Murphy *English Grammar in Use* (Unit 107).
- **Common errors:** "She sings good" → "She sings well"; "I feel well" vs "I feel good" — both correct, different meaning.
- **Exam relevance:** Moderate.

## Section Structure

### 1. Introduction: "You Speak Good English" — Wrong?
- Almost everyone says it. Here's why it's technically wrong — and why it's spreading.

### 2. The Basic Rule
- **Good** = adjective → modifies nouns: a good book, she is good, he is a good student.
- **Well** = adverb → modifies verbs: she sings well, he plays well, they performed well.

### 3. The Health Exception: "Well" as Adjective
- "Well" can be an adjective meaning "in good health": "I feel well," "She is well."
- "I feel good" = general state of happiness/health.
- "I feel well" = specifically, I am not ill.

### 4. Linking Verbs and "Good"
- After linking verbs (feel, look, smell, taste, sound), adjectives are correct: "It looks good," "That smells good."
- These are NOT adverbs — they're subject complements.
- "The food tastes good" (not "well" — unless you mean the food has the ability to taste!)

### 5. "Well" in Informal Speech
- "She speaks English well" (correct) vs "She speaks good English" (informal, widely used).
- In formal writing and exams, use the standard rule.

### 6. Common Mistakes
- "He plays good" → "He plays well"
- "She is well at math" → "She is good at math" (adjective + preposition)
- "I did good on the test" → "I did well on the test"

### 7. Quick Practice
- 5 sentences — choose good or well.
- Answer key.

### 8. Summary
- Good = adjective (nouns). Well = adverb (verbs). Well = adjective only for health.
