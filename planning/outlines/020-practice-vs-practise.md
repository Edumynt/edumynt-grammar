## Research / Grounding Notes

- **Core concept:** British English: "practice" = noun, "practise" = verb. American English: "practice" for both.
- **Key rule (BrE):** I need more practice (noun). I need to practise daily (verb).
- **References:** Swan *Practical English Usage* (entry on practice/practise); Oxford style guide.
- **Common errors:** "I need to practice" (AmE correct, BrE should be "practise"); "daily practise" (BrE should be "practice").
- **Exam relevance:** Moderate — Indian exams follow British conventions.
- **Overlap warning:** Related to #019 (advice/advise) — same pattern.

## Section Structure

### 1. Introduction: Two Spellings, One Word?
- British English keeps them separate. American English doesn't. Which should you use?

### 2. The British Rule
- **Practice** (noun): "Daily practice is essential." "The doctor's practice."
- **Practise** (verb): "I practise every day." "She practises law."

### 3. The American Simplification
- American English uses "practice" for both noun and verb.
- "I practice every day" (AmE) = "I practise every day" (BrE).

### 4. What Indian Exams Expect
- Indian English follows British conventions.
- Use "practice" for noun, "practise" for verb in formal writing and exams.

### 5. The -C / -S Pattern (Recap)
- Noun: -c (practice, advice, licence).
- Verb: -s (practise, advise, license).

### 6. Common Mistakes
- "I need more practise" (BrE) → "more practice" (noun)
- "I practise daily" (BrE, correct as verb)
- "Daily practise" → "daily practice" (noun)
