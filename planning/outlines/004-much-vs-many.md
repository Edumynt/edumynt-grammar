## Research / Grounding Notes

- **Core concept:** "Many" modifies countable nouns; "much" modifies uncountable (mass) nouns. The distinction is grammatical, not semantic.
- **Key rule:** If you can count it (one book, two books) → many. If you can't (water, information) → much.
- **References:** Quirk et al. §5.18–5.20; Swan *Practical English Usage* (entries on much/many); Murphy *English Grammar in Use* (Units 87–88).
- **Common errors:** "many water," "much people," "much books."
- **Exam relevance:** Very common in error spotting and fill-in-the-blanks.

## Section Structure

### 1. Introduction: Two Words for "A Lot"
- Both mean a large quantity — but they're not interchangeable.
- The rule depends on the type of noun.

### 2. Countable vs Uncountable Nouns — Quick Refresher
- Countable: things you can count individually (books, cars, ideas).
- Uncountable: things treated as a mass or whole (water, advice, furniture).
- Brief note: some nouns are both depending on meaning (a glass / some glass).

### 3. The Rule
- **Many** + plural countable nouns: many books, many students, many opportunities.
- **Much** + uncountable nouns: much water, much time, much information.

### 4. In Questions and Negatives
- "Much" is natural in questions and negatives: "How much time?" "I don't have much money."
- "Many" works in all positions: "How many books?" "I don't have many books."
- In affirmative statements, "a lot of" / "lots of" is more natural than "much": "I have a lot of money" (not "I have much money" — grammatically correct but formal/awkward).

### 5. "Too Much," "So Much," "As Much"
- "Too much" + uncountable: too much noise, too much work.
- "Too many" + countable: too many mistakes, too many people.
- "So much / so many," "as much / as many" — same pattern.

### 6. Common Mistakes
- "many water" → "much water"
- "much people" → "many people"
- "much information" (correct — information is uncountable)
- "many furnitures" → "much furniture" (furniture is uncountable)
