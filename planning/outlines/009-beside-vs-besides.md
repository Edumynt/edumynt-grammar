## Research / Grounding Notes

- **Core concept:** "Beside" is a preposition meaning "next to / at the side of." "Besides" is a preposition or adverb meaning "in addition to / moreover / apart from."
- **Key rule:** beside = physical proximity; besides = addition or "anyway."
- **References:** Swan *Practical English Usage* (entry on beside/besides); Merriam-Webster usage notes.
- **Common errors:** "beside from" (wrong — use "besides" or "apart from"); using "beside" when "besides" is meant.
- **Exam relevance:** Moderate — appears in error spotting and word choice questions.
- **Overlap warning:** None — unique topic.

## Section Structure

### 1. Introduction: One Letter Changes Everything
- "She sat beside me" vs "Besides, I don't want to go."
- Same root, completely different meanings.

### 2. "Beside" — Next To
- Preposition of place: beside the river, beside me, sit beside her.
- Physical or metaphorical proximity: "He was beside himself with anger" (idiom — extremely angry).

### 3. "Besides" — In Addition To / Moreover
- Preposition: "Besides English, she speaks French." (= in addition to)
- Adverb: "I don't want to go. Besides, it's raining." (= anyway / moreover)
- "Besides" can also mean "except" in some contexts: "No one besides John knew."

### 4. The "S" Makes the Difference
- Think: the "s" in "besides" stands for "supplementary" / "also."
- "Beside" = static position. "Besides" = adding something.

### 5. Common Mistakes
- "Beside English, she speaks Hindi" → "Besides English, she speaks Hindi"
- "Besides the river, there was a tree" → "Beside the river, there was a tree" (physical location)
- "Beside from" → not a standard phrase; use "besides" or "apart from"

### 6. Quick Practice
- 5 sentences — choose beside or besides.
- Answer key.

### 7. Summary
- Beside = next to (position). Besides = in addition to / moreover (addition).
