## Research / Grounding Notes

- **Core concept:** "Fewer" is used with countable nouns; "less" with uncountable nouns. This parallels many/much.
- **Key rule:** fewer + plural count noun; less + mass noun.
- **References:** Quirk et al. §5.18; Swan *Practical English Usage* (entry on fewer/less); Merriam-Webster usage note on "less."
- **Common errors:** "less items," "less people," "10 items or less" (supermarket signs).

## Section Structure

### 1. Introduction: The Supermarket Sign Everyone Argues About
- "10 items or less" — is it wrong?
- The answer is more interesting than grammar pedants admit.

### 2. The Standard Rule
- **Fewer** + countable plural nouns: fewer students, fewer problems, fewer opportunities.
- **Less** + uncountable nouns: less water, less time, less energy.

### 3. Why People Say "Less" for Everything
- Historical note: "less" has been used with count nouns since Old English (it's the comparative of "little").
- The "fewer/less" distinction was imposed by prescriptivist grammarians in the 18th century.
- In natural speech, "less" with count nouns is widespread and accepted by most modern grammarians.

### 4. When "Less" with Count Nouns Is Acceptable
- With time, money, distance, and weight: "less than 5 miles," "less than 2 hours," "less than ₹500."
- These are conceptually mass quantities even though the unit is countable.
- "Less than 10 people" — widely accepted in modern usage.

### 5. When to Stick with "Fewer"
- In formal writing and exams: "fewer students passed" (not "less students").
- When the noun is clearly countable and plural.

### 6. Common Mistakes
- "less people" → "fewer people" (formal) / "less people" (informal, widely used)
- "fewer water" → "less water"
- "less mistakes" → "fewer mistakes"
