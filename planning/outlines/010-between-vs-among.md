## Research / Grounding Notes

- **Core concept:** Traditional rule: "between" for two; "among" for three or more. Modern usage: "between" is acceptable for more than two when describing distinct, individual relationships.
- **Key rule:** between = distinct items (two or more); among = undifferentiated group.
- **References:** Quirk et al. §6.22; Swan *Practical English Usage* (entry on between/among); Merriam-Webster usage note.
- **Common errors:** "among you and I" → "between you and I" (or better: "between you and me").
- **Exam relevance:** High — frequently tested.

## Section Structure

### 1. Introduction: Two or More?
- "Between you and me" vs "among friends" — the traditional rule is simple.

### 2. The Traditional Rule
- **Between** for two: between you and me, between tea and coffee.
- **Among** for three or more: among the students, among the crowd.

### 3. The Modern Exception: "Between" with More Than Two
- "Choose between tea, coffee, and juice" — acceptable because the items are distinct.
- "The treaty was signed between France, Germany, and Italy" — correct because it's about reciprocal relationships.
- Key: use "between" when items are **individually identified**; "among" when they're part of an **undifferentiated mass**.

### 4. "Among" vs "Amongst"
- "Amongst" is a variant of "among" — more common in British English, slightly more formal.
- No difference in meaning.

### 5. Common Mistakes
- "among you and I" → "between you and me"
- "between the crowd" → "among the crowd" (undifferentiated group)
- "among the three options" → "between the three options" (distinct items — both can work)

### 6. Quick Practice
- 5 sentences — choose between or among.
- Answer key.

### 7. Summary
- Two distinct items → between. Undifferentiated group → among. Multiple distinct items → between is fine.
