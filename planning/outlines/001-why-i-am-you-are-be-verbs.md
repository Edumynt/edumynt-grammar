## Research / Grounding Notes

- **Core concept:** The verb "to be" is the most irregular verb in English. Its present tense forms (am, is, are) and past tense forms (was, were) change based on grammatical person (1st, 2nd, 3rd) and number (singular/plural).
- **Key rule:** I → am / was; He/She/It → is / was; You/We/They → are / were.
- **References:** Quirk et al. *A Comprehensive Grammar of the English Language* (§3.8–3.10); Huddleston & Pullum *The Cambridge Grammar of the English Language* (Ch. 3); Murphy *English Grammar in Use* (Units 24–25).
- **Common errors:** "I is" (common in learner English and some dialects); "you was" (non-standard); "he am" (L1 interference).
- **Exam relevance:** Frequently tested in error spotting and fill-in-the-blanks.
- **Overlap warning:** None — unique topic.

### 1. Introduction: The Strangest Verb in English
- Hook: "I am happy" but "you are happy" — why does the verb change?
- "Be" is the only English verb with three distinct present-tense forms.
- Brief note: this is called **subject–verb agreement** (or **conjugation**).

### 3. Why "Be" Is So Irregular
- Historical note: "be" is a **suppletive** verb — its forms come from three different Old English roots (wesan, bēon, am/earun).
- This is why no other English verb behaves this way.
- Fun fact: most languages have an irregular "be" verb.

### 4. The Simple Rule: Match the Subject
- Rule: The form of "be" depends on the **subject**, not the complement.
- "The boys **are** tall" (subject = boys → plural → are).
- "The problem **is** the boys" (subject = problem → singular → is).
- Emphasize: find the subject first, then choose the form.

### 5. Negative and Question Forms
- Negative: add "not" → am not, is not (isn't), are not (aren't).
- Question: invert subject and verb → "Are you ready?" "Is she coming?"
- Contractions in spoken/informal English.

### 6. "Be" as a Helping Verb
- Progressive tenses: "She **is** running" (be + present participle).
- Passive voice: "The letter **was** written" (be + past participle).
- Brief mention — link to dedicated posts on passive voice and tenses.

### 7. Common Mistakes
- "I is" → "I am"
- "You was" → "You were"
- "He don't" → "He doesn't" (related but different verb — worth noting)
- "There is many people" → "There are many people" (the real subject follows "there")
- Dialectal forms: "I were" in some British dialects (non-standard but culturally present).
