## Research / Grounding Notes

- **Core concept:** The choice between "a" and "an" depends on the **initial sound** (not letter) of the following word. "A" before consonant sounds; "an" before vowel sounds.
- **Key rule:** It's phonetic, not orthographic. "An hour" (silent h), "a university" (yoo- sound).
- **References:** Quirk et al. §5.10–5.12; Swan *Practical English Usage* (entries 1–3, articles); Huddleston & Pullum §5.6.
- **Common errors:** "an historic" (debated — British English sometimes uses "an" before pronounced h in older usage); "a hour" (wrong); "an unique" (wrong — "unique" starts with /j/ sound).
- **Exam relevance:** Very common in error spotting and fill-in-the-blanks.
- **Overlap warning:** Related to #003 (definite article) and #085 (advanced article usage) — keep this one focused on a/an only.

## Section Structure

### 1. Introduction: It's About Sound, Not Spelling
- Hook: Why do we say "a apple" is wrong but "an apple" is right? And why "a university"?
- The rule is simpler than you think — and also trickier.

### 2. The Basic Rule
- "A" before words that begin with a **consonant sound**.
- "An" before words that begin with a **vowel sound** (a, e, i, o, u sounds).
- Examples: a book, an apple, a cat, an egg.

### 3. The Sound Rule in Action — Tricky Cases
- Silent "h": an hour, an honest man, an heir.
- Pronounced "h": a house, a hotel, a hero (in most modern English).
- "U" words: a university (/juː/), a European (/jʊ/), a useful book — all consonant sounds.
- "O" words: an open door, but a one-way street (/wʌn/ starts with /w/ sound).
- Abbreviations: an FBI agent (eff-bee-eye starts with /ɛ/ vowel sound), a NASA scientist (starts with /n/).

### 4. The "Historic" Debate
- British English tradition: "an historic event" (h was often silent or weakly pronounced).
- American English: "a historic event" (h is pronounced).
- Modern consensus: both are acceptable; follow your variety of English.
- Mention: "an historian" vs "a historian" — same logic.

### 5. Common Mistakes
- "a hour" → "an hour"
- "an useful" → "a useful"
- "a honest person" → "an honest person"
- "an one" → "a one"
- "a MBA" → "an MBA" (em-bee-ay starts with vowel sound).

### 6. Why This Rule Exists
- Phonological reason: "an" prevents two vowel sounds from colliding (hiatus). "A apple" forces an awkward pause.
- Historical note: "an" is the older form; "n" dropped before consonant sounds over time in English.
